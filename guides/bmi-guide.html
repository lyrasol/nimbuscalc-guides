BMI Guide: Understanding Your Body Mass Index

Body Mass Index (BMI) is a widely used measure to estimate whether a person has a healthy body weight for their height. This guide explains how to calculate BMI, interpret the results, and understand its limitations.

What is BMI?

BMI is a simple calculation using a person's height and weight. It's an inexpensive and easy screening method for weight categories that may lead to health problems. The formula is:

BMI = weight (kg) / height² (m²)
Or for imperial units: BMI = (weight (lb) / height² (in²)) × 703

BMI Categories

The World Health Organization defines these standard categories:

  • Underweight: BMI less than 18.5
  • Normal weight: BMI 18.5 – 24.9
  • Overweight: BMI 25 – 29.9
  • Obese: BMI 30 or greater

How to Calculate Your BMI

You can calculate it manually using the formula above, or use our free BMI calculator. Simply enter your height and weight, and the tool will instantly show your BMI and category.

Example: A person weighing 70 kg with a height of 1.75 m has a BMI of 70 ÷ (1.75 × 1.75) = 22.9, which falls in the "Normal weight" range.

Limitations of BMI

While BMI is useful for population studies, it has limitations for individuals:

  • Doesn't distinguish between muscle and fat mass (athletes may have high BMI but low body fat).
  • Doesn't account for fat distribution.
  • May not be accurate for pregnant women, elderly, or certain ethnic groups.

For a more complete picture, consider using additional tools like body fat calculator, waist-to-hip ratio, or ideal weight calculator.

Why Track Your BMI?

Maintaining a healthy weight reduces risk of heart disease, diabetes, and other conditions. Regularly checking your BMI can help you monitor progress toward your health goals.
